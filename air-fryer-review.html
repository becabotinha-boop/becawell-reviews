Frequently Asked Questions

Can it cook a whole chicken, or just pieces?

Pieces work better than whole birds. A spatchcocked chicken under 4 pounds can fit, but four to six bone-in thighs or a split breast is the more practical use case. You get better air circulation around individual pieces, which means crispier skin and more even cooking. If whole roast chicken is a weekly staple for you, a dedicated rotisserie air fryer might be a better fit.

How loud is it during operation?

It runs at roughly the same volume as a medium box fan — noticeable but not disruptive. You can hold a normal conversation in the kitchen while it's running. It's quieter than most stand mixers and noticeably quieter than a range hood fan at high speed. If you're in a small apartment with thin walls and noise-sensitive neighbors, it's worth knowing it does produce a consistent hum.

Does it actually use less oil, or is that marketing?

For most foods, a light spray or toss with a teaspoon of oil gets you results that are genuinely comparable to deep-frying in terms of texture and crispiness. Frozen foods with existing oil content often need no added oil at all. It's not zero-oil cooking — completely dry proteins and vegetables can come out tough — but you're using a fraction of what deep frying or even pan frying requires. The reduction is real.

How does it handle reheating versus a microwave?

For anything with a crust, coating, or texture — pizza, fries, fried chicken, spring rolls — it's significantly better than a microwave. Reheat at 350°F for 3 to 5 minutes and you get food that's actually crispy again, not steamed-soft. For soups, stews, or rice dishes, a microwave is still faster and equally effective. The air fryer becomes the go-to for anything where texture matters.

Is it worth buying if I already have an Instant Pot?

They do different things well enough that having both makes sense for a lot of home cooks. The Instant Pot is better for braises, beans, soups, and anything that benefits from moisture and pressure. The air fryer is better for anything where you want browning, crispiness, or fast dry heat. Many people end up using the Instant Pot for batch cooking proteins and the air fryer for weeknight reheating and vegetables. There's real overlap in convenience, but not much overlap in what each does best.
