Frequently Asked Questions

Can it actually fit enough food for a family of four?

Yes, for most proteins and vegetables. Four bone-in chicken thighs or two full salmon fillets fit flat in the basket without stacking. For sides, a pound of Brussels sprouts or two medium zucchini diced up fits in a single layer. Where it gets tight is if you're trying to do a protein and a vegetable simultaneously — you'll usually do those as back-to-back batches rather than simultaneously, which still saves significant time compared to the oven.

Does it actually make food crispy, or is it more like a mini convection oven?

Functionally it is a convection oven in a compact form, but the basket design and the concentrated airflow produce noticeably crispier exteriors than a standard convection setting on a full-size oven. Chicken skin renders and crisps well. Frozen fries come out with actual snap. Vegetables get charred edges rather than steaming. The honest caveat: it won't replicate deep-fry texture exactly, but for oil-free or minimal-oil cooking, the results are much closer than any other countertop method.

How hard is it to clean?

Easier than most cooking equipment, which is a meaningful part of why people actually keep using it. The basket and the inner tray both go in the dishwasher — that covers the surfaces food actually touches. The exterior wipes down with a damp cloth. If you're cooking something fatty like chicken thighs, there will be some splatter inside the unit body, which you'll want to wipe out with a paper towel every few uses. It takes two minutes. It's genuinely not a deterrent.

What foods does it NOT do well?

Anything with a wet batter — traditional beer-battered fish, tempura — won't work because the batter drips before it sets. Delicate leafy greens will blow around from the fan. Large cuts that need slow, low-heat cooking (a pork shoulder, a beef roast) need more time and space than this unit handles well. And baking — cakes, muffins, bread that needs to rise — doesn't translate well to the basket format. Stick to roasting, reheating, and anything you'd normally do at 375–400°F in the oven.

Is it loud? I live in an apartment and cook late.

The fan noise is real and worth knowing about. It runs consistently during the cook cycle and is audible from the next room — roughly comparable to a bathroom exhaust fan. It's not disruptive at normal hours, but if you're cooking at midnight and noise is a genuine concern, it's worth factoring in. The unit auto-shuts off at the end of the timer and the fan stops with it, so there's no residual noise once the cycle ends.
