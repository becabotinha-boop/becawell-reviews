Frequently Asked Questions

Will it handle an onion cleanly without me cutting it into a specific shape first?

Yes, but with one step: halve the onion first, then place the flat side down on the grid before pressing. A whole uncut onion is too round to sit stably and will shift when you press. Once halved, it goes through cleanly in a single firm press. Most soft-to-medium vegetables — peppers, zucchini, celery, mushrooms — need no special prep before chopping.

How hard is it to press down? I have wrist and hand pain.

It requires a firm, deliberate press — similar to pressing down on a stapler with your palm, not your fingers. Soft vegetables like peppers and mushrooms need less force; firmer ones like raw carrots and sweet potato need more. If you have significant hand or wrist pain, the denser vegetables may still be a challenge. Softer vegetables and onions, though, are quite manageable for most people with mild joint discomfort.

How many blade inserts does it come with, and can I buy extras?

Most Pro Vegetable Chopper versions ship with 4 to 6 blade inserts — typically a small dice (around 1/2 inch), a medium dice, a larger chunk size, and one or two slice options. The exact count varies by retailer listing, so check the product page before buying. Replacement inserts are generally available separately, though the unit itself holds up well enough that most buyers don't need replacements within the first year or two of regular use.

Is it actually dishwasher safe, or will that ruin the blades?

The manufacturer rates it dishwasher safe on the top rack, and most reviewers report no issues with that method. That said, dishwashing will dull the blades slightly faster over time compared to hand-washing — same as with any knife. For a tool you're using daily, rinsing the blade grid by hand takes under 30 seconds and keeps it sharper longer. The container and body hold up fine in the dishwasher without any caveats.

What vegetables does it struggle with?

The main limitations are dense, hard root vegetables: raw sweet potato, butternut squash, raw beets, and large raw carrots. These cut, but they require significantly more force than softer vegetables and may need a second press for a clean result. Tomatoes can be inconsistent — very ripe tomatoes compress rather than cut cleanly. Everything else in a standard vegetable rotation (onions, peppers, zucchini, celery, cucumber, broccoli stems) works well.
