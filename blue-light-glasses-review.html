Frequently Asked Questions

Will these work if I already use f.lux or my monitor's night mode?

Yes. f.lux and night mode warm your screen's color temperature—helpful, but they don't block the blue light physically. These glasses do both: they warm the incoming light AND filter the wavelengths your eyes are most sensitive to. Most users report that combining all three (monitor setting + software + glasses) is better than any single fix. But even without f.lux, these work.

Do they make everything look orange? Will I look weird on video calls?

No and no. Mid-range blue light glasses use a subtle amber tint, not the heavy orange of cheap pairs. On video calls, you look normal. Your monitor colors shift slightly warmer, but it's subtle enough that you adjust within minutes. If you do color-sensitive work (design, photo editing), the tint is minimal enough that you can work accurately. You might adjust your monitor's white balance slightly, but that's it.

How long do they last? Do the lenses degrade?

The anti-reflective coating is durable for 2–3 years of daily wear. The blue light filtering is built into the lens material, not a coating, so it doesn't wear off. Most people keep these for 3+ years. The main wear is the frames themselves—hinges, nose pieces. For the price, treating them as a 2-3 year tool is realistic. If you take care of them (case, regular cleaning), they last longer.

Will they help if I already have migraines or dry eyes?

They may help if your migraines are triggered by screen strain and blue light. If you already have chronic dry eyes, these alone won't fix it—you need lubricating drops and possibly an eye doctor visit. But combining these glasses with artificial tears makes a measurable difference for many people. Always check with your eye doctor if migraines or dry eyes are severe.

Do I need a prescription version, or can regular blue light glasses work over my contacts?

Regular blue light glasses work great over contacts. If you wear glasses, ask your optometrist about adding blue light filtering to your prescription—it's usually $30–50 more and gives you a single pair instead of two. If you're not ready to upgrade your Rx, these over-the-counter pairs are a low-cost way to test whether blue light blocking helps you before investing in prescription lenses.
