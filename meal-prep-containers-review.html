Frequently Asked Questions

Are the lids actually leak-proof for lunch bags?

Not fully. The snap-lock lids are secure against spills when the container is upright, and they hold reasonably well in a bag as long as the contents aren't liquid-heavy. For dry foods, grains, and proteins they're fine. For soups, dressings, or anything with a lot of moisture, you'll want to pack them upright or use a separate small container for the liquid component. They're not rated as fully leak-proof and shouldn't be trusted on their side in a stuffed bag.

Can I actually put these in the microwave with the lid on?

Yes, with the tabs lifted. The lids have four snap tabs — pop two opposing tabs open before microwaving to vent steam and you're set. Don't microwave with all four tabs closed; pressure builds up and the lid can pop. The containers themselves handle microwave heat without warping at normal reheat times (1–3 minutes). They're not rated for extended high-heat cooking, just reheating — which is all most people need them for.

Do the divided containers actually keep foods separate?

For dry or semi-dry foods, yes — the dividers are tall enough to keep a chicken breast on one side and roasted broccoli on the other without mixing. Where it breaks down is with anything saucy or wet. A portion of pasta with marinara will seep under the divider if it sits for a day. The practical workaround most people use is keeping sauced items in single-compartment containers and using the divided trays for dry prep layouts.

How do 20 containers actually fit in a normal fridge?

Better than you'd expect, because the uniform sizing is what makes them stackable. Full containers stack three or four high without shifting. Empty containers nest inside each other in a cabinet drawer. Most people keep 6–8 filled containers in the fridge at a time (a week of lunches), not all 20. The rest store flat or stacked empty. If your fridge has a dedicated produce drawer you're not fully using, that's usually where a full week of prepped meals fits cleanly.

How do they hold up after 6 months of daily use?

Based on owner reviews, the containers themselves hold up well — no cracking or warping with regular microwave and dishwasher use. The lids are the more common failure point over time; the snap tabs can lose some tension after heavy use, which means they close but don't snap as firmly. Most buyers report this happening after 8–12 months of daily cycling. At the price point, buying a second set at that point is still cheaper than most comparable alternatives.
