Frequently Asked Questions

Does the red light setting actually help with melatonin, or is that just marketing?

The underlying science is real. Long-wavelength red light (around 630–700nm) does not suppress melatonin production the way blue and white light does. Colors available's deep red mode operates in that range. It won't manufacture sleepiness on its own, but it removes a key source of circadian disruption during the window before sleep. Most users notice a difference within 5–10 days of consistent use.

Can I use it as my only bedroom light, or does it need to supplement overhead lighting?

It works well as your only light source for the final 60–90 minutes before bed, which is exactly the use case it's designed for. At brightness level 6–7 in warm yellow mode, there's enough light to read, get ready for bed, or have a conversation comfortably. It's not intended to replace overhead lighting for tasks like getting dressed in the morning — the cool white mode helps there, but it's not a substitute for a full room light.

Will the light bother a partner who goes to sleep earlier than me?

At the lower end of the brightness range (levels 1–3) in red or deep amber mode, the light is directional enough and dim enough that most partners report it doesn't disturb them. That said, everyone's sensitivity is different. The auto-fade timer helps — you can set it to dim to off before your partner's lightest sleep phase. Several reviewers specifically mention using it successfully in shared bedrooms.

How long does the battery last on a single charge?

Runtime varies by brightness level. At the mid-range (level 5, warm amber), expect roughly 8–10 hours of continuous use before needing a charge. At the lower settings used for the final approach to sleep, runtime extends significantly. For most users, charging every 2–3 days is typical with a standard bedtime routine. The USB-C port means charging while in use is straightforward if you want to leave it plugged in permanently.

Is this worth it if I've already tried melatonin and it didn't work?

Possibly, yes — because it addresses a different mechanism. Melatonin supplementation works by adding the hormone externally; this works by removing the light-based suppression that stops your body from producing it naturally. They're complementary rather than competing approaches. Many reviewers specifically mention buying colors available after supplements failed them, and finding better results. If your issue is environment and timing rather than a clinical deficit, the light approach has a good track record.
