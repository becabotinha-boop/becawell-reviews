What the Revlon Volumizer Actually Does

The Revlon One-Step is a hot air brush — one tool that combines the airflow of a hair dryer with the tension and heat of a round brush. The oval barrel is the key design choice here. Unlike a perfectly cylindrical brush, the oval shape creates a natural lifting angle at the root, which is where volume actually comes from. You're not just drying your hair — you're training it to sit higher. The bristle mix matters too. The nylon pins grip and separate sections while the boar bristles smooth the hair shaft as you pull through. The result is that your hair dries with the cuticle lying flat, which is what creates shine rather than that matte, slightly rough finish you get from air-drying or a basic dryer. Ionic technology releases negatively charged ions that counteract the positive static built up during drying — in practical terms, this means significantly less frizz in the finished style. There's also a cool shot button that sets the style once you've achieved the shape you want, locking in the curve and volume before you move to the next section. The 1100-watt motor is adequate for most hair types, though it's worth noting upfront that it's not the highest wattage option available.

Real-World Use: What It's Like Day-to-Day

Picture a Tuesday morning. You've washed your hair, towel-dried it to damp — not soaking, not mostly dry, damp — and you pick up the Volumizer. Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Revlon Volumizer work on thick hair?

It can, but with caveats. Users with thick hair consistently report longer drying times and recommend working in smaller sections than the instructions suggest. The 1100-watt motor isn't designed for high-density hair, so results vary. If your hair is thick and coarse, tools with 1400+ watts and a larger barrel may give you better volume and faster results. For thick but fine-stranded hair, the Volumizer tends to perform better.

How wet should my hair be before using the Volumizer?

Damp, not soaking. Most users get the best results when hair is towel-dried to about 70–80% dry before picking up the brush. Starting too wet means the motor has to work harder and the styling takes longer; starting too dry reduces your ability to shape the root lift. If you air-dry for 10 minutes after toweling off, you'll hit the ideal starting point. Some users with fine hair find they can start slightly wetter for more volume hold.

Will the bristles snag or pull my hair?

On detangled hair, the mixed nylon and boar bristles move through smoothly without significant pulling. Where users run into trouble is starting on tangled or knotted hair — detangle thoroughly with a wide-tooth comb first. A few reviewers with very fine or fragile hair mention some breakage at the ends after months of daily use, which is worth noting if your hair is already chemically treated or heat-damaged. Using a heat protectant spray beforehand is recommended regardless.

How long does the blowout last — will it hold all day?

For most hair types, yes. The volume at the root holds well through a full workday in normal conditions — reviewers in low-humidity climates report results lasting into the evening. In high humidity, results vary more. The ionic technology helps reduce frizz but won't fully hold a style in very humid weather the way a finishing product would. A light-hold spray after styling extends the result noticeably if longevity is your priority.

Is the Revlon Volumizer safe for color-treated or highlighted hair?

Yes, with the same precautions you'd apply to any heat styling tool. Use a heat protectant spray before each use — this applies whether your hair is color-treated or not. The temperature settings top out at a moderate heat level (lower than many traditional dryers at max), which is generally considered safer for color-treated hair than high-heat tools. Users with highlighted or bleached hair report no unusual damage when using a protectant, but daily use on very fragile hair requires extra care.
