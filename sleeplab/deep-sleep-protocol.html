The Deep Sleep Protocol — 7 Steps Before Any Supplement

The behavioral protocol I built from sleep research before trying anything in a bottle. Most people see meaningful improvement from steps 1–4 alone.

✓ 100% Free — No Supplement Required

I tracked my sleep with a wearable for 3 months before trying any supplement. These are the 7 interventions that moved the needle most — ranked by impact based on my own data and the published research.

Why Behavior First

Most people jump to supplements before they've addressed the behavioral factors that are often causing the problem. A melatonin pill won't fix blue light exposure at 11pm. Ashwagandha won't compensate for an inconsistent sleep schedule. Supplements work best when the behavioral foundation is already in place — and for many people, fixing the foundation is enough.

I'm not anti-supplement. I tested Resurge and found it useful. But this protocol comes first — and it's free.

In my tracker data, implementing steps 1–3 increased my average deep sleep duration by 34 minutes per night — before I changed anything else.

The 7-Step Protocol (Ranked by Impact)

1

Bedroom Temperature: 65–68°F (18–20°C) HIGHEST IMPACT

Your body needs to drop its core temperature to initiate deep sleep. A warm room prevents this. This was the single highest-impact change in my data — 23 extra minutes of deep sleep per night after dropping my room from 72°F to 66°F. Use a fan, lower the thermostat, or use a cooling mattress pad. Non-negotiable.

2

Light Exposure: Zero Blue Light 90 Minutes Before Bed HIGH IMPACT

Blue light (phones, laptops, TVs) suppresses melatonin production by 50–80% in studies. Use Night Mode on devices, wear blue-light-blocking glasses after 9pm, or simply put screens down. I used the glasses — compliance is easier than willpower-based "no phone" rules.

3

Sleep Schedule Consistency: Same Wake Time Every Day HIGH IMPACT

Your circadian clock runs on your wake time, not your bedtime. Set an alarm and don't move it — even weekends. Irregular wake times fragment sleep architecture and reduce deep sleep. This is harder than it sounds if you're used to sleeping in on weekends. The payoff is significant: in my data, consistent wake time improved sleep efficiency from 71% to 84% over 3 weeks.

4

Caffeine Cutoff: No Caffeine After 1pm HIGH IMPACT

Caffeine has a 5–7 hour half-life. A 3pm coffee still has half its caffeine active at 9pm. A 5pm coffee has half its caffeine at 11pm. This is silently wrecking deep sleep for most people. I moved my cutoff from 4pm to 1pm and saw immediate deep sleep improvement in my tracker data. Tea counts, including green tea.

5

Pre-Sleep Routine: 20-Minute Wind-Down Ritual MODERATE IMPACT

Your nervous system needs a transition signal — a consistent sequence that tells your brain "sleep is coming." Mine: dim lights, herbal tea, 10 minutes reading physical book (no screen), light stretching. The specific activities matter less than the consistency. Do the same thing every night.

6

Alcohol: Eliminate or Cut Off 3 Hours Before Bed MODERATE IMPACT

Alcohol sedates — it doesn't sleep. It suppresses REM sleep and fragments the second half of the night, causing early waking and poor recovery. If you drink, cut off 3 hours before sleep. Even one glass 2 hours before bed measurably reduces REM in tracker data. This surprised me the most when I saw my own numbers.

7

Stress Offloading: "Brain Dump" Journal Before Bed MODERATE IMPACT

Cortisol from unresolved stress keeps your nervous system activated at bedtime. A simple 5-minute journal — writing down tomorrow's tasks and any unresolved thoughts — moves the "open loops" from your working memory to paper, reducing the mental activation that delays sleep onset. Harvard sleep research supports this; it reduced sleep onset time by average 9 minutes in one study.

It's designed to support the hormonal side of sleep — specifically deep sleep duration and HGH release — not as a replacement for the behavioral foundation.
