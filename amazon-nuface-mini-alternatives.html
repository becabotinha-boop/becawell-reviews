Frequently Asked Questions

Is microcurrent technology safe to use at home?

Yes, for most healthy adults, FDA-cleared microcurrent devices designed for home use are considered safe when used as directed. The current levels in consumer devices — typically 100–400 µA — are far below clinical thresholds and well below the threshold needed to cause tissue damage. That said, there are contraindications you should be aware of: microcurrent devices are not recommended for individuals with pacemakers, epilepsy, active implants, or open wounds on the treatment area. Pregnant individuals should also consult a healthcare provider before use. If you have any underlying medical conditions affecting your skin or nervous system, check with a dermatologist or GP before starting a home microcurrent routine. Always patch-test on a small area first, and start at the lowest intensity setting available on your device. Individual results and sensitivities vary — what one person finds comfortable, another may find intense.

How long does it take to see results from a microcurrent facial device?

Most clinical literature and consumer feedback points to an 8–12 week window for noticeable, lasting results from consistent use. Some users report visible changes in contour and firmness within 3–4 weeks, but individual results vary significantly based on age, baseline skin laxity, hormonal factors, diet, hydration, and how consistently the device is used. The NuFace protocol, for example, recommends a 5-minute daily session for the first 60 days — often called the "activation phase" — before dropping to a 2–3 times per week maintenance schedule. Skipping sessions frequently during that first phase is the most common reason users report disappointing results. It's also worth noting that microcurrent stimulates muscle memory and collagen production over time, meaning results are cumulative — not immediate or guaranteed after a fixed number of sessions.

Can I use a microcurrent device with my existing skincare routine?

In most cases, yes — with some important caveats. Microcurrent devices require a conductive medium between the device and your skin. Devices like the NuFace Mini need a water-based gel primer; without it, you won't get effective energy transfer and may experience skin drag or discomfort. The Foreo Bear Mini is more flexible and works with most water-based serums you already own, which can reduce ongoing costs. You should avoid using microcurrent immediately after applying active ingredients like retinoids, AHAs, BHAs, or vitamin C at high concentrations, as the energy transfer can increase penetration in ways that may cause irritation. The safest approach is to use your device on clean, freshly washed skin with only the recommended conductive gel, then apply your actives afterward. Always check your specific device's guidelines for compatibility — and when in doubt, consult a licensed esthetician or dermatologist about integrating any new device into your current skincare regimen.
