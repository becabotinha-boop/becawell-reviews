The 6 Main Causes of Neuropathy

Neuropathy isn't one condition — it's a description of nerve damage from many different causes. The cause determines the mechanism, which determines which interventions are likely to help. This distinction matters enormously when evaluating treatment options.

1. Diabetic Neuropathy (Most Common)

High blood glucose over time damages the small blood vessels that supply nerves (vasa nervorum), starving nerve tissue of oxygen and nutrients. This is the most studied form of neuropathy and has the strongest evidence base for natural interventions — particularly Alpha Lipoic Acid (which reduces oxidative stress in nerve tissue) and B-vitamin complex (which supports nerve repair and conduction).

2. Nutritional Deficiency Neuropathy

B12 deficiency is a direct cause of peripheral neuropathy — it's required for myelin sheath synthesis. B1 (thiamine) deficiency causes a specific neuropathy pattern. Both are potentially improvable if caught early. Get your B12 levels checked before trying supplements — if deficient, this alone may support symptom improvement.

3. Compression / Entrapment Neuropathy

Mechanical pressure on a nerve (carpal tunnel, herniated disc, piriformis syndrome) damages the nerve differently — through physical compression rather than metabolic damage. Supplements have limited evidence for this type. The primary interventions are structural: physical therapy, ergonomics, and sometimes surgery.

4. Chemotherapy-Induced Neuropathy

Certain chemotherapy agents (taxanes, platinum compounds) directly damage peripheral nerves. This is a distinct mechanism from diabetic neuropathy. Some evidence exists for Acetyl-L-Carnitine and omega-3s, but evidence is weaker than for diabetic neuropathy.

5. Autoimmune Neuropathy

The immune system attacks nerve tissue (as in Guillain-Barré syndrome or CIDP). This requires medical management. Natural interventions play a supportive role at best.

6. Idiopathic Neuropathy (Unknown Cause)

Approximately 30% of peripheral neuropathy cases have no identified cause. This is frustrating but common. The approach here is to address all potentially contributing factors: nutritional status, blood sugar, oxidative stress, sleep quality, and inflammation.

What This Means for Supplements
